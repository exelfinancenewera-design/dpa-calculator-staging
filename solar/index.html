Solar in 2026 — straight answers

Sourced, current, and free of installer spin.

Is the 30% federal solar tax credit still available in 2026?
No. Per the IRS, the Residential Clean Energy Credit (§25D) "is not available for any property placed in service after December 31, 2025." Systems bought and installed in 2026 do not receive the 30% federal credit. Leased/PPA systems may still benefit indirectly through the provider's business credit — ask what they pass through (EnergySage notes competitive providers should). IRS source
What solar incentives still exist in 2026?
State-level programs survive. Example: New York's solar equipment credit is 25% of cost up to $5,000 (tax.ny.gov). Many states offer property-tax or sales-tax exemptions, utility rebates, or net-metering bill credits. Select your state above — the calculator itemizes what we could verify, with official links.
Is solar still worth it without the federal credit?
It depends mostly on your electricity rate. At May-2026 EIA rates, a California household paying 33.25¢/kWh can still see payback well under 10 years at 2026's ~$2.60/W average prices; a 13¢/kWh state takes far longer. That's exactly the honest math this page computes for your inputs.
How accurate is the production estimate?
We use your state's average solar resource at latitude tilt (NLR — formerly NREL — Perez/SUNY dataset) with a conservative 0.80 performance ratio and 0.5%/yr panel degradation. Your roof's direction and shading will move the result; treat this as a well-sourced estimate, not a site survey.
Why doesn't this calculator ask for my address or email?
Because it isn't a lead-capture form. Address-first calculators exist to sell your contact details to installer sales teams. This one runs entirely in your browser, instantly, and shows its sources.
What about net metering?
To stay conservative we cap savings at your own usage — we assume no payment for exported surplus. If your utility credits exports at retail rate, your real savings may be higher; if you're on a reduced export rate (like California's NBT), sizing near 100% of usage — as the auto-size does — keeps this estimate representative.

Methodology & sources

The formula, in the open
Annual production (kWh) = system kW × state solar resource at latitude tilt (kWh/m²/day, NLR/NREL Perez-SUNY dataset, state centroid) × 365 × 0.80 performance ratio.
Year-1 savings = min(production, your usage) × your electricity rate. Production degrades 0.5%/yr.
Gross cost = kW × 1,000 × $/W (EnergySage state marketplace averages, Jul 17 2026). Net cost = gross − verified state/utility incentives. No federal credit is applied — the §25D credit ended Dec 31, 2025 (IRS).
Payback = the year cumulative savings crosses net cost (interpolated). 25-yr net savings = 25-yr cumulative savings − net cost. Flat electricity rate assumed (conservative — rates rose ~6% year-over-year to May 2026 per EIA).
Data sources

· Installed cost per watt: EnergySage marketplace state averages, updated Jul 17, 2026.
· Solar resource: NLR (formerly NREL) Solar Resource API, annual average at latitude tilt, state geographic centroid.

· State incentives: each shown with its official source link in the stack table. States without a link show $0 — check your state energy office and utility.
